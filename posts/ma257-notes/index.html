Summary

Below, $p$ is a prime. There are few big parts to the module:

  1. Numbers (Chapter 1.1 - 1.3)

    • Division & Divisibility
    • Primes
      • Fundamental theorem of arithmetic (FTA)
    • Computing GCD / LCM
      • Euclidean algorithm
      • Extended Euclidean algorithm
      • Bezout’s lemma

  2. Basic congruences (Chapter 1.4 - 1.5)

    • Addition, multiplication, “division”
    • Chinese remainder theorem (CRT)
      • Numeric (with modulos)
      • Algebraic (with rings)
    • Linear congruence(s)
      • Single: $ax \equiv b \pmod{m}$
      • System: $x \equiv a_i \pmod {b_i}$
    • Descent
      • Infinite descent

  3. More congruences (Chapter 1.5 - 1.9)

    • Euler totient function
      • Definition: $\varphi(n) = \left|\left(\mathbb{Z} / n\mathbb{Z}\right)^{\times}\right|$
      • Identities: $\varphi(pq) = \varphi(p)\varphi(q)$ and $\sum_{d \mid n} \varphi(d) = n$
    • Euler’s theorem & Fermat’s little theorem
      • Simplify: $a^{\varphi(n)} \pmod{n}$, $a^{p - 1} \pmod{p}$, $13^{200} \pmod {19}$
    • Wilson’s theorem
      • Reduce $(p - 1)! \pmod{p}$
    • Primitive roots
      • Existence, number of them
      • Primitive roots in $\textrm{mod} \,\, p^k$
    • Quadratic residues
      • Legendre symbol $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$
      • Euler’s criterion $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \equiv a^{(p - 1) / 2} \pmod p$
      • Quadratic reciprocity $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{\frac{p - 1}{2}\frac{q - 1}{2}}$
      • And its supplementary laws for $\left(\frac{-1}{p}\right)$ and $\left(\frac{2}{p}\right)$

  4. Lattices (Chapter 2.1 - 2.2)

    • Minkowski’s theorem
      • Statement
      • Proving Two-square theorem, four-square theorem

  5. Gaussian integers (Chapter 2.3)

    • Gaussian integers
      • Definition: $\mathbb{Z}[i] = a + bi$
      • Norm: $N(a + bi) = a^2 + b^2$
      • Multiplicative norm
      • Bezout’s lemma applies
    • Gaussian primes
      • Classification (up to units): All primes $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ or $\alpha$ where $N(\alpha)$ is prime
      • FTA into Gaussian primes
      • Factorisation algorithm: For $p \mid N(\alpha)$,
        • If $p = 2$ then $1 + i \mid \alpha$
        • If $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ then $p \mid \alpha$
        • Otherwise, $p = a^2 + b^2 = (a + bi)(a - bi)$ are primes

  6. Pythagorean triples (Chapter 2.4 - 2.5)

    • Parametrisation: $(x, y, z) = (u^2 - v^2, 2uv, u^2 + v^2)$
    • Solving $ax^2 + by^2 = cz^2$
      • Criterion: $(bc, ac, -ab)$ is a quadratic residue mod $(a, b, c)$.

  7. $p$-adic numbers (Chapter 2.6 - 2.7)

    • $p$-adic integers
      • Definition: Sequence $(x_1, x_2, \cdots) \in \mathbb{Z}_p$ where $x_{n + 1} \equiv x_n \pmod{p^n}$
      • Termwise addition and multiplication
      • Units in $\mathbb{Z}_p$: All numbers where $x_1 \neq 0$
    • Hensel’s lemma
      • Statement: If $v_p(f’(x)) = k$ and $v_p(f(x)) \geq 2k + 1$, then $f$ has a root $y$ where $y \equiv x \pmod{p^{k + 1}}$
      • Actually computing the roots.

  1. A positive integer can be written as $d = \overline{d_k d_{k-1} \cdots d_2 d_1 d_0} = d_0 + 10d_1 + 10^2d_2 + \cdots + 10^kd_k$ in base $10$. Then,

$$ d \equiv \sum_{i = 0}^k 10^i \cdot d_i \equiv \sum_{i = 0}^k 1^i \cdot d_i = \sum_{i = 0}^k d_i \pmod{k} $$

Hence, $d$ is divisible by $9$ if and only if its sum of digits is.

  1. We compute $g$ using the Euclidean Algorithm.

$$ \def\arraystretch{1.5} \begin{array}{lll} g &= \gcd(1038, 2628) \\ &= \gcd(1038, 552) &\because 2628 = 2 \times 1038 + 552 \\ &= \gcd(486, 552) &\because 1038 = 1 \times 552 + 486 \\ &= \gcd(486, 66) &\because 552 = 1 \times 486 + 66 \\ &= \gcd(24, 66) &\because 486 = 7 \times 66 + 24 \\ &= \gcd(24, 18) &\because 66 = 2 \times 24 + 18 \\ &= \gcd(6, 18) &\because 24 = 1 \times 18 + 6 \\ &= 6 \end{array} $$

Next, we reverse the process to solve the equation:

$$ \def\arraystretch{1.5} \begin{array}{lll} 6 &= 24 - 1 \times 18 \\ &= 24 - 1 \times (66 - 2 \times 24) \\ &= 3 \times 24 - 1 \times 66 \\ &= 3 \times (486 - 7 \times 66) - 1 \times 66 \\ &= 3 \times 486 - 22 \times 66 \\ &= 3 \times 486 - 22 \times (552 - 1 \times 486) \\ &= 25 \times 486 - 22 \times 552 \\ &= 25 \times (1038 - 1 \times 552) - 22 \times 552 \\ &= 25 \times 1038 - 47 \times 552 \\ &= 25 \times 1038 - 47 \times (2628 - 2 \times 1038) \\ &= 121 \times 1038 - 47 \times 2628 \end{array} $$

Which gives us one solution $(x, y) = (121, -47)$ to $1038x + 2628y = 6$. Finally, we compute that $\frac{1038}{6} = 173$ and $\frac{2628}{6} = 438$. Hence, all solutions are parametrised by

$$ (x, y) = \left(121 + 438t, -47 - 173t\right), \quad t \in \mathbb{Z} $$

  1. We argue by contradiction. Suppose that $\gcd(a, b) = d$ and $\gcd(a’, b’) = d’ > 1$. Then, we can write $a’ = d’a’’$ and $b’ = d’b’’$, where $a’’, b’’$ are integers. Substituting the definitions of $a’$ and $b’$ and rearranging, we get that $a = dd’a’’$ and $b = dd’b’’$. This means that $\gcd(a, b) = \gcd(dd’a’’, dd’b’’) = d$. We see that $dd’$ divides both $dd’a’’$ and $dd’b’’$, meaning $dd’ \mid \gcd(dd’a’’, dd’b’’) = d$. However, $d’ > 1$ gives $dd’ > d$, implying that $dd’ \nmid d$, contradiction.
  1. We claim that the inverse of $7$ mod $55$ is $8$. To see this, we check that $7 \cdot 8 = 56 \equiv 1 \pmod{55}$. Hence, $7x \equiv 5 \pmod{55} \iff 8 \cdot (7x) \equiv 8 \cdot 5 \pmod{55} \iff x \equiv 40 \pmod{55}$. For $35x \equiv 10 \pmod{55}$, we see that $\gcd(35, 55) = \gcd(10, 55) = 5$. Therefore, we divide the numbers by $5$ to get $7x \equiv 2 \pmod{11}$. From here, we get $8 \cdot (7x) \equiv 8 \cdot 2 \pmod{11} \iff 56x \equiv 16 \pmod{11} \iff x \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$.

The thing to remember here is that the equation $ka \equiv kb \pmod{c}$ is equivalent to $a \equiv b \pmod{\frac{c}{\gcd(c, k)}}$.

  1. The number of zeros at the end of a number $n$ is the minimum of the number of $2$ and $5$ that divides $n$, i.e. $\min(v_2(n), v_5(n))$. We compute the quantities $v_2(1000!)$ and $v_5(1000!)$ separately.

For $v_2(1000!)$, write $1000! = 1 \times 2 \times \cdots \times 999 \times 1000$. Recall that $v_2(ab) = v_2(a)v_2(b)$ (which can be seen by considering the prime factorisation). Therefore, $v_2(1000!) = \sum_{i = 1}^{1000} v_2(i)$. Next, out of the numbers $1, 2, \cdots, 1000$, the numbers $2, 4, 6, \cdots, 1000$ will contribute $1$ to the sum, since $2$ divides these numbers. However, we also consider the numbers $4, 8, 12, \cdots, 1000$ which contributes an extra $1$ to the sum, since $4 = 2^2$ divides these numbers, so we must count them extra. Continuing, we consider the numbers $1 \cdot 2^k, 2 \cdot 2^k, 3 \cdot 2^k, \cdots, \lfloor \frac{1000}{2^k} \rfloor \cdot 2^k$ that are all divisible by $2^k$ and contribute an extra $1$ to the sum (compared to the previous $k$). Therefore,

$$ \begin{align*} v_2(1000!) &= \sum_{k = 1}^{\infty} \underbrace{\lfloor \frac{1000}{2^k} \rfloor}_{\text{contribution from} \, 2^k} \\ &= \lfloor \frac{1000}{2} \rfloor + \lfloor \frac{1000}{4} \rfloor + \cdots + \lfloor \frac{1000}{512} \rfloor &\because 2^{10} = 1024 > 1000 \\ &= 500 + 250 + 125 + 62 + 31 + 15 + 7 + 3 + 1 \\ &= 994 \end{align*} $$

Similarly, we compute $v_5(1000!)$ with the same argument to get

$$ \begin{align*} v_5(1000!) = \lfloor \frac{1000}{5} \rfloor + \lfloor \frac{1000}{25} \rfloor + \lfloor \frac{1000}{125} \rfloor + \lfloor \frac{1000}{625} \rfloor = 200 + 40 + 8 + 1 = 249 \end{align*} $$

We get $v_{10}(1000!) = \min(994, 249) = 249$, meaning $1000!$ has $249$ trailing zeroes.

  1. We show that $n^5 \equiv n \pmod{30}$. By Chinese Remainder Theorem, it suffices to show that $n^5 \equiv n \pmod{2, 3, 5}$ separately. Firstly, note that $0^5 \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$ and $1^5 \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$, so $n^5 \equiv n \pmod{2}$. Next, by Fermat’s little Theorem, we see that $n^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, so $n^5 = n \cdot (n^2)^2 \equiv n \pmod{3}$. We also see that $n^4 \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$, so $n^5 \equiv n \pmod{5}$. Hence, $30 \mid n^5 - n$.

Similarly, we show $42 \mid n^7 - n$ by proving $n^7 \equiv n \pmod{2, 3, 7}$. Firstly, note that $0^7 \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$ and $1^7 \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$, so $n^7 \equiv n \pmod{2}$. Next, by Fermat’s little Theorem, we see that $n^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, so $n^7 = n \cdot (n^2)^3 \equiv n \pmod{3}$. We also see that $n^6 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$, so $n^7 \equiv n \pmod{7}$. Hence, $42 \mid n^7 - n$.

There are other methods to prove this. For example, writing $n^5 - n = n(n - 1)(n + 1)(n^2 + 1)$, we immediately see that $6 \mid n^5 - n$. Proving $5 \mid n^5 - n$ is more annoying but it is just casework on $n \pmod{5}$.

  1. Suppose that there is an integer solution $(x, y, z)$ to the equation $x^2 - 3y^2 = 5z^2$. Without loss of generality, we can assume that $\gcd(x, y, z) = 1$, since $(dx)^2 - 3(dy)^2 = 5(dz)^2 \implies x^2 - 3y^2 = 5z^2$. Note that $(0^2, 1^2, 2^2, 3^2, 4^2) = (0, 1, 4, 9, 16) \equiv (0, 1, 4, 4, 1) \pmod{5}$. We take the given equation modulo $5$ to get

$$ x^2 + 2y^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{5} $$

From the first observation, we see that $(x^2, 2y^2) \equiv (0, 0), (0, 2), (0, 8), (1, 0), (1, 2), (1, 8), (4, 0), (4, 2), (4, 8) \pmod{5}$. These correspond to $x^2 + 2y^2 \equiv 0, 2, 3, 1, 3, 4, 4, 1, 2 \pmod{2}$ respectively. Therefore, the only solution to $x^2 + 2y^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$ is when $(x^2, 2y^2) \equiv (0, 0) \pmod{5}$, i.e. when $x \equiv y \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$.
