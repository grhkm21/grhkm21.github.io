Investigating Pythagorean Triplets (1)

Notes of Pythagorean Triplets

This is a short article re-deriving the Tree of (primitive) Pythagorean Triplets, as well as a few interesting generalisations.

Firstly, let’s begin with the equation $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$. A triplet $(x, y, z)$ satisfying the equation is called a Pythagorean Triplet, abbreviated PT. If we further have that $\gcd(x, y, z) = 1$, then we say that $(x, y, z)$ is a primitive PT, abbreviated PPT.

Theorem 1: There are infinitely many PT.

Proof (Incomplete): Let $x$ be any odd number at least $3$, say $x = 13$. Square it to get $x^2 = 169$. Now, we find two consecutive integers that add up to it. We can see that $84$ and $85$ works, and hence $(x, y, z) = (13, 84, 85)$ works - indeed, $13^2 + 84^2 = 7225 = 85^2$. You can repeat this for any odd $x \geq 3$.

Theorem 2: There are infinitely many PPT.

Proof: Take the PT constructed above, say $(x, y, y + 1)$. Notice that $\gcd(y, y + 1) = 1$, since no numbers divide $y$ and $y + 1$ at the same time other than $1$. Therefore, $\gcd(x, y, y + 1) = \gcd(x, 1) = 1$, and the PT is primitive.

However, the method above does not construct every solution. For example, $(8, 15, 17)$ is missed, since $15$ and $17$ are not consecutive. Therefore, we want to find a way to enumerate all the solutions. Looking at the equation, it seems that we cannot progress further. We can try factorization by rearranging the terms, but after $x^2 = z^2 - y^2 = (z - y)(z + y)$, we are stuck again.

Let’s look at some small PPT for inspiration:

$$(3, 4, 5), (5, 12, 13), (7, 24, 25), (8, 15, 17), (9, 40, 41), \cdots$$

One observation is that $z < x + y$ for all of them. This is indeed true since $z^2 = x^2 + y^2 < (x + y)^2$. An idea we might try is to use the substitution $z \mapsto x + y - w$. Substituting, we get

$$\begin{align*} x^2 + y^2 &= z^2 \\ x^2 + y^2 &= (x + y - w)^2 \\ x^2 + y^2 &= x^2 + y^2 + w^2 + 2xy - 2wx - 2wy \\ 0 &= w^2 - 2wx - 2wy + 2xy \\ w^2 &= 2w^2 - 2wx - 2wy + 2xy \\ w^2 &= 2w(w - x) - 2y(w - x) \\ w^2 &= 2(w - x)(w - y) \\ w^2 &= 2(x - w)(y - w) \end{align*}$$

Wow, we got an interesting factorization this time! To simplify our notation, let’s do one more substitution: By substituting $x \mapsto x + w$ and $y \mapsto y + w$, our left-hand side will simplify to $2xy$. Let’s rename the variable a bit, and our new substitution becomes $f: (x, y, z) \mapsto (u + w, v + w, u + v + w)$:

$$\begin{align*} (u + w)^2 + (v + w)^2 &= (u + v + w)^2 \\ w^2 &= 2uv \end{align*}$$

This is a very simple equation! Let’s try it out. For $w = 6$, we get $uv = 18$. Then if we take $(u, v) = (2, 9)$, we get $(u + w, v + w, u + v + w) = (8, 15, 17)$, which is indeed a PT!

Alright, so this is a cool equation, but it doesn’t help us construct every solution yet, so let’s investigate it further.

Let’s take our solution of $8^2 + 15^2 = 17^2$ and $(u, v, w) = (2, 9, 6)$. How can we possibly generate more solutions from this? Here is the trick: taking the negative of any of $x, y, z$ also works. That is, we have

$$\begin{align*} 8^2 + 15^2 &= 17^2 \\ (-8)^2 + 15^2 &= 17^2 \\ 8^2 + (-15)^2 &= 17^2 \\ (-8)^2 + (-15)^2 &= 17^2 \end{align*}$$

I know this looks trivial, but let’s continue this thought. From these PT, let’s compute the $(u, v, w)$ parameters. To do this, let’s solve the following equations:

$$\begin{cases}\begin{align*} u + \qquad w &= x \\ v + w &= y \\ u + v + w &= z \end{align*}\end{cases} \implies (u, v, w) = (-y + z, -x + z, x + y - z $$

Now, what does $(x, y, z) = (\pm 8, \pm 15, \pm 17)$ give?

$$\begin{align*} (x, y, z) = (8, 15, 17) &\implies (u, v, w) = (2, 9, 6) \\ (x, y, z) = (-8, 15, 17) &\implies (u, v, w) = (2, 25, -10) \\ (x, y, z) = (8, -15, 17) &\implies (u, v, w) = (32, 9, -24) \\ (x, y, z) = (-8, -15, 17) &\implies (u, v, w) = (32, 25, -40) \end{align*}$$

Well, now we are getting some new numbers. However, these still don’t help us get new solutions - recovering the $(x, y, z)$ for these values of $(u, v, w)$ will just give us the left-hand side! However, there is one final trick.

Recall that these values satisfy $w^2 = 2uv$. This means that if $(u, v, w)$ is a solution, then so is $(u, v, -w)$! Let’s try that on the values above:

$$\begin{align*}(u, v, w) &= (2, 9, {\color{red}{-}} 6) \implies (x, y, z) = (-4, 3, 5) \\ (u, v, w) &= (2, 25, {\color{red}{+}} 10) \implies (x, y, z) = (12, 35, 37) \\ (u, v, w) &= (32, 9, {\color{red}{+}} 24) \implies (x, y, z) = (56, 33, 65) \\ (u, v, w) &= (32, 25, {\color{red}{+}} 40) \implies (x, y, z) = (72, 65, 97)\end{align*}$$

Woohoo! We got some new solutions! Note that the first row gives a “smaller” solution. This is because this method produces new solutions only when $w > 0$. Now, let’s generalise this technique. For a given solution of $(x, y, z)$, we do the following

$$\begin{alignat*}{5} (x, y, z) &\to (\pm x, \pm y, z) &&\to (u, v, w) &&\to (u, v, -w) &&\to (x’, y’, z’) \\\\ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &\to \begin{pmatrix} -x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} -y + z \\ x + z \\ -x + y - z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} -y + z \\ x + z \\ x - y + z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} x - 2y + 2z \\ 2x - y + 2z \\ 2x - 2y + 3z \end{pmatrix} \\\\ \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &\to \begin{pmatrix} x \\ -y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} y + z \\ -x + z \\ x - y - z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} y + z \\ -x + z \\ -x + y + z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} -x + 2y + 2z \\ -2x + y + 2z \\ -2x + 2y + 3z \end{pmatrix} \\\\\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &\to \begin{pmatrix} -x \\ -y \\ z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} y + z \\ x + z \\ -x - y - z\end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} y + z \\ x + z \\ x + y + z \end{pmatrix} &&\to \begin{pmatrix} x + 2y + 2z \\ 2x + y + 2z \\ 2x + 2y + 3z \end{pmatrix}\end{alignat*}$$

Note that this gives us a way to generate new PT from old ones. We can also write this in a more compact way using matrix multiplication, which is how we summarize below:

Theorem: Let $M_1, M_2, M_3$ be the matrices

$$ M_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 & 2 \\ 2 & -1 & 2 \\ 2 & -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, M_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 & 2 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 \\ -2 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, M_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then if $\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix}$ is a Pythagorean Triplet, so is $M_1\vec{v}, M_2\vec{v}$ and $M_3\vec{v}$.

Finally, I would state the following result without proof. I might add the proof in the future:

Theorem (Berggren, 1934):

  1. If $\vec{v}$ is a primitive Pythagorean Triplet (PPT), so is $M_1\vec{v}, M_2\vec{v}$ and $M_3\vec{v}$.
  2. For each PPT $\vec{v}$, there exists a unique finite product of the matrices, say $M = \prod_{i=1}^k M_{j_i}$, $1\leq j_i\leq 3$, such that

$$ \vec{v} = M\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

What this means is that these matrices generate every primitive Pythagorean Triplet. Since the word of matrices is unique, this also means that the set of PPT is a partially ordered set (poset)!

Finally, here are some exercises for the reader:

  • Apart from the transformation $w \mapsto -w$, we also have $(u, v) \mapsto (-u, -v)$. Verify that it does not generate larger solutions.
  • Prove (1) of Berggren’s Theorem.
  • Prove (2) of Berggren’s Theorem. (Hint: Uniqueness is easy. Prove existence by contradiction.)
